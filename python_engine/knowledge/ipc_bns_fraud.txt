Chapter I: Cheating and Fraud (IPC & BNS equivalents)
Section 420 of the Indian Penal Code (IPC) [Equivalent to Section 318 of the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS), 2023] defines Cheating and dishonestly inducing delivery of property. If a person is deceived into delivering property, money, or goods to another person under fraudulent pretenses, it constitutes an offense under this section. The punishment involves imprisonment for up to seven years and a fine.
This section is widely applicable to financial scams, fake investment schemes, online defrauding, and instances where services paid for were deliberately never rendered.

Chapter II: Criminal Breach of Trust
Section 405 of the IPC [Equivalent to Section 316 of the BNS, 2023] defines Criminal Breach of Trust. This occurs when a person is entrusted with property (or dominion over property), and they dishonestly misappropriate or convert it to their own use. This is common in cases where a business partner, employee, or family member steals or misuses funds entrusted to them for a specific purpose.

Chapter III: Criminal Intimidation
Section 503 of the IPC [Equivalent to Section 351 of the BNS, 2023] defines Criminal Intimidation. Threatening another with any injury to their person, reputation, or property, with the intent to cause alarm or force them to do an act which they are not legally bound to do, is punishable. This covers blackmail, severe threats, or goons threatening eviction.

Where to approach:
For serious offenses like Cheating or Extortion, a victim must file a First Information Report (FIR) at the jurisdictional Police Station under Section 154 of the CrPC [Section 173 of the BNSS, 2023]. If the police refuse to register an FIR, the victim can send the complaint to the Superintendent of Police via Registered Post, or file a private criminal complaint before the Judicial Magistrate under Section 156(3) CrPC [Section 175(3) BNSS].
